AI startup mines failed trial data to predict which patients will actually respond to treatment

Verge Labs has built an AI model that re-analyses datasets from failed clinical trials to identify patient sub-populations that did respond to treatments which flopped in aggregate. The company targets neurology — the category with the highest trial failure rates — arguing it can revive abandoned drug candidates by enrolling the right patients in future trials.

Why it matters: Patient stratification is the highest-leverage AI application in drug development. If this works at scale, it could meaningfully cut the 90%+ failure rate in CNS trials — a category where India's pharma generics sector and CRO industry are increasingly active participants.

Merck bets $510M (biobucks) on AI drug design platform Protillion

Merck has signed a collaboration and license agreement with Protillion, an AI-native drug design company, worth up to $510 million in milestone payments. The deal pairs Protillion's generative design platform with Merck's drug discovery expertise and biology data to drive new candidate generation.

Why it matters: Big pharma is doubling down on AI drug design partnerships after early-stage deals proved the model. This is the benchmark deal that India's AI drug discovery startups will be measured against when approaching global pharma partnerships.

FDA clears Colorado to import prescription drugs from Canada to bring prices down

The FDA has approved Colorado's application to import certain prescription drugs from Canada — the first state-level importation approval. The move is part of a broader push to reduce drug prices through expanded import channels and marks a significant policy shift from the FDA's historically restrictive stance on importation.

Why it matters: This is an early real-world case study in how drug pricing reform actually happens at the system level. The state-by-state importation model is one that health policy observers in India — watching drug pricing battles between MNCs and the NPPA — will track for comparative lessons.

ARCH-backed Neumora scraps its lead depression drug; cuts 35% of workforce

Neumora Therapeutics — one of the more closely watched depression drug programmes, backed by ARCH Venture Partners — has stopped its lead candidate after disappointing trial results and is laying off 35% of its staff. Separately, enGene has halved its workforce, underscoring a broader biotech headcount contraction.

Why it matters: Depression drugs have a brutal clinical failure rate, and Neumora was a well-resourced bet by sophisticated investors. The layoffs tracker across biotech this year suggests the sector is in a capital-efficiency phase — relevant context for health tech investors and for CROs serving the space.
