The Economist profiles the world's most AI-optimistic economists — and where they work

The Economist surveys economists who believe AI will fundamentally reshape productivity and growth curves — a cohort it labels "AI-pilled." Most are not in academic positions but in tech companies, hedge funds, and central bank research departments. Their models project productivity growth significantly above mainstream consensus.

Why it matters: Understanding the intellectual scaffolding of AI-optimism is useful for anyone in strategy or founder work — especially when engaging with investors or boards operating from very different priors. Knowing who these economists are and what they're projecting is part of navigating the AI discourse credibly.

Japan and South Korea are outpacing the West in science output growth — with India watching

The latest Nature Index shows China still leads global scientific output with 22% growth year-over-year, but Japan and South Korea are rising fastest among the top 10, outpacing the US, Germany, and UK in contribution growth rates. The West's research dominance is no longer a given.

Why it matters: India's own research output is growing but undercounted in global rankings due to institutional and publication-quality gaps. The East Asia surge is a data point for Indian universities and R&D policymakers making the case for more investment — and a competitive pressure signal for India's tech talent positioning.
