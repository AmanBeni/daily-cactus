Chilling the Body With Drugs Could Limit Brain Damage From Stroke

Pharmacologically cooling core body temperature may put brain cells into a hibernation-like state following a stroke, preserving them from damage. Animal studies show significant reduction in infarct volume, opening a path to neuroprotection outside ICU settings.

Key takeaway: Drug-induced hypothermia for stroke could work as well as physical cooling — and be far more deployable in resource-limited settings.

Why it matters: India loses 650,000 people to stroke annually. A treatment that can be deployed at district hospitals without specialised equipment would be transformative for outcomes.

Oldest Known Plague Outbreak Killed Hunter-Gatherer Children 5,000 Years Ago

DNA evidence from Siberia reveals that plague bacteria devastated a community of hunter-gatherers more than 5,000 years ago, challenging the idea that major disease outbreaks required farming and large populations to emerge.

Key takeaway: Plague predates farming — challenging core assumptions about disease emergence and population density requirements for epidemic spread.

Why it matters: Understanding ancient disease dynamics matters for pandemic preparedness modelling. The assumption that dense populations are prerequisite for epidemic emergence may need revision.

Our Brains Have Their First Thoughts Surprisingly Early in Life

By the time we’re born, our brains have all the hardware in place to form thoughts — and possibly even some conscious awareness. New research suggests the first neural patterns resembling thought may emerge in the third trimester, weeks earlier than previously assumed.

Key takeaway: The neural architecture for thought appears earlier in fetal development than assumed — pushing back the timeline for when minds begin.

Why it matters: The science of early cognition has implications for neonatal care, early childhood education, and the longstanding philosophical debate about consciousness and personhood.

Autism and ADHD Are Rising Because Diagnostic Criteria Are Widening, Not Just Because More Cases Exist

A study of 140,000 people finds that the broadening of diagnostic criteria for autism and ADHD explains the sharp rise in diagnoses — but that doesn’t mean too many people are being labelled. Wider criteria capture real cases that were previously missed.

Key takeaway: The autism/ADHD rise is primarily definitional, not purely epidemiological — but the wider criteria are capturing genuinely underserved cases.

Why it matters: India has among the lowest neurodevelopmental diagnosis rates globally — not because the conditions are rare but because awareness and infrastructure are limited. This trend will eventually reach India as awareness grows.

Pigeons Lock Their Eyes in Place When Flying — A Surprising Avian Visual Strategy

Bird-mounted headsets and backpacks revealed that pigeons stabilise their eyes relative to the ground while in flight — an unexpected strategy for maintaining visual clarity in a moving environment. The finding challenges assumptions about avian visual processing.

Key takeaway: Pigeons use gaze stabilisation relative to the ground rather than to their heads — a novel visual strategy not previously observed in birds.

Why it matters: Understanding biological vision systems informs computer vision and autonomous navigation research. Bio-inspired algorithms remain a productive source of ideas for robotics and drone navigation.
