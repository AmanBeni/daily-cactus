Global Economics

PBOC Signals Shift to Fed-Style Overnight Rate Framework

China's central bank has signalled a major potential shift in how it sets monetary policy — moving toward an overnight rate as its primary policy tool, similar to how the US Federal Reserve operates with the federal funds rate. The change would bring greater transparency and predictability to Chinese rate-setting, reducing the opacity that has made yuan-denominated instruments unattractive to foreign investors.

Key takeaway: China is building the monetary infrastructure for yuan internationalisation by adopting the Fed's most trusted policy tool.

Why it matters: A more transparent PBOC framework could accelerate yuan's reserve currency ambitions and reshape how Asian trade — including India-China commerce — is invoiced. Indian exporters and importers would face a more predictable renminbi, reducing hedging costs and uncertainty in bilateral trade.

Bank of Japan Raises Rates to 31-Year High as Iran War Inflation Persists

The Bank of Japan raised its benchmark rate to 1% — the highest since 1995 — citing persistent inflation pressure compounded by the Iran-Hormuz energy shock. The US Federal Reserve and Bank of England held rates steady in the same week, with the Fed waiting for US labor market data and the BoE citing post-Hormuz uncertainty. The BOJ move signals the definitive end of Japan's ultra-loose monetary era.

Key takeaway: Japan joining the global tightening cycle means the last major safe-haven for cheap global capital has closed — yen carry trades are now genuinely expensive.

Why it matters: The yen carry trade has been a major source of capital for emerging markets. Higher BOJ rates mean these trades unwind, pulling capital from EM equity and debt markets — including India. RBI may need to act defensively if EM outflows accelerate.

ECB: US-Iran Peace Accord Won't Be Enough to Stop Further Rate Hikes

European Central Bank officials are signalling that even a successful US-Iran peace deal and full Hormuz reopening won't be sufficient to reverse the energy shock embedded in eurozone inflation. With natural gas storage still below seasonal norms and industrial energy contracts locked at war-level prices through year-end, the ECB sees further tightening as likely regardless of geopolitical outcomes.

Key takeaway: Energy inflation in Europe is structural, not just geopolitical — even peace in Iran doesn't fix the price contracts already signed at crisis levels.

Why it matters: Higher ECB rates and European recession risk are the key transmission channels for global economic slowdown to India. European demand for Indian IT services and pharma exports could soften if the eurozone enters recession — a risk materialising in Q3 2026 corporate earnings.

China Embeds Anti-Sanctions Clauses in Financial Law as Economy Slumps

Chinese Vice Premier He Lifeng announced that Beijing will embed anti-sanctions provisions directly into its financial laws — a counter-measure against what officials called "unreasonable" Western economic pressure. The move comes as China's economy faces slowing growth, property sector stress, and reduced access to Western capital markets, pushing Beijing toward financial self-reliance as a strategic imperative.

Key takeaway: China is legalising financial warfare readiness — embedding counter-sanctions into domestic law signals a permanent shift toward economic self-reliance.

Why it matters: Indian companies operating in both US and Chinese markets face increasing legal complexity as China and the West build incompatible financial law regimes. The dual-compliance burden will become a competitive cost for Indian multinationals operating across this divide.

PBOC Launches New Tool to Boost Yuan Use by Foreign Central Banks

China's central bank announced new money-market instruments designed to make it easier for foreign central banks and sovereign wealth funds to hold and transact in yuan. The tool provides overnight liquidity in renminbi to qualified foreign institutions, reducing the friction that has historically made yuan reserve allocation unattractive compared to dollar or euro instruments.

Key takeaway: China is systematically eliminating the friction points that have prevented yuan from becoming a practical reserve currency — this is the plumbing layer.

Why it matters: India's RBI holds minimal yuan reserves. But as India-China trade expands and PBOC tools improve yuan liquidity, the case for partial yuan reserve allocation will strengthen — especially given India's interest in reducing dollar dependency. This is a long game, but it's moving faster than expected.
