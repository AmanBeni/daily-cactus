Monash University Puts a Complete Photonic Circuit on a Single Chip — a Milestone for Light-Based Computing

Researchers at Monash University have built the first on-chip system that generates, routes, and reads information carried by light all within a single integrated device, published in Nature Photonics in June 2026. The chip uses ultra-thin, few-atom materials paired with specially engineered nanostructures. Until now, researchers could do any one of these three things on a chip — not all three together.

Why it matters: Photonic computing promises fundamentally faster data transmission and dramatically lower energy consumption than silicon electronics — the missing piece has been full integration. This research closes that gap. As AI inference demands grow, the question of whether to run it on electrons or photons becomes an engineering race; Monash just put photons one step closer to winning it.

Chinese Firm Claims Optical Chip Is 1,000× Faster Than Nvidia GPUs for AI — But Yields Tell a Different Story

A Chinese semiconductor company is claiming its new optical quantum chip achieves AI processing workloads 1,000 times faster than Nvidia GPUs, with the firm reportedly producing 12,000 wafers per year. The key caveat: yields — the proportion of functional chips per wafer — remain low, raising serious questions about commercial viability at scale.

Why it matters: Take the 1,000× headline with appropriate skepticism (low yield equals not production-ready). But the direction of travel matters: China is investing heavily in photonic and quantum computing precisely to route around Nvidia's hardware dominance. For India's semiconductor ambitions, this is the lane worth watching — not the transistor race, but the photonics race.

New Light-Based Switch Could Significantly Reduce Chip Energy Use — A Key Step for Photonic AI Accelerators

Researchers have developed a light-based switching mechanism that substantially reduces chip energy consumption by controlling optical signals more efficiently than conventional electronic switches. The design enables faster switching speeds while reducing heat dissipation — two of the biggest engineering bottlenecks in scaling AI compute infrastructure.

Why it matters: Energy consumption is one of the most critical constraints on AI infrastructure growth — data centres are already bumping against power grid limits worldwide. Photonic switching of this kind is the upstream technology that makes next-generation AI accelerators possible; it matters not as a product today but as the building block of products in 2028–2030.
