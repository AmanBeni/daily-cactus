Rainbow Crops raises $11M to scale AI-guided simultaneous editing of multiple crop genes

Rainbow Crops has raised $11.25 million to advance its "multiplex gene editing" platform — a technique for editing several genes in a crop simultaneously, guided by AI optimisation. The company is in talks with a major seed company on a corn collaboration and is expanding into other crops. Multiplex editing can potentially produce climate-resilient, high-yield varieties faster than conventional breeding.

Why it matters: India's agricultural future depends on crop varieties that can handle rising temperatures and erratic monsoons. Multiplex AI-guided editing, if it crosses from pilot to commercial seed scale, could be a key tool. Partnerships with global seed companies will determine whether Indian farmers get access on viable terms — or whether this remains a technology for well-capitalised agricultural systems.

French alt-meat startup SWAP Food collapses into liquidation after raising €100M+

SWAP Food, a Paris-based startup known for whole-cut chicken fillet alternatives, has entered judicial liquidation — the French equivalent of forced dissolution — despite having raised over €100 million ($116 million) in debt and equity since 2020. The failure is one of the most visible exits yet in the alternative proteins sector, which has seen multiple high-profile stumbles in the past two years.

Why it matters: The alt-protein hype cycle has definitively met gravity. For Indian agritech founders and investors, this is a useful check on "food of the future" narratives: SWAP had the funding and the product — the market just wasn't there at the required scale and price point. Consumer adoption, not technology, remains the binding constraint.

A $1.5M startup is building real-time nutrient testing for farms — no lab required

Picketa Systems has raised $1.5 million to deploy handheld sensors that test soil and plant tissue for nutrient levels in the field, removing the need to send samples to a distant laboratory. The company says real-time data closes the "fertilizer loop," enabling precision application instead of blanket spreading.

Why it matters: India's smallholders routinely over-apply certain fertilizers while starving crops of micronutrients — a structural inefficiency affecting both yield and soil health. Low-cost in-field sensors of this kind could shift behaviour at scale if they're cheap enough and if extension services back them. Watch for Indian market entry or copycat startups.

AI-optimised plasma could make green ammonia competitive with fossil-fuel fertilizer, startup claims

Faraday Earth is using AI-optimised plasma reactors to produce ammonia from air and water — a potential replacement for the Haber-Bosch process, which currently accounts for roughly 2% of global energy consumption. The startup claims it can reach a levelised cost of ~$500 per tonne, which would put green ammonia within striking distance of conventional grey ammonia pricing.

Why it matters: India imports a significant share of its fertilizer. A scalable green ammonia pathway would reshape India's fertilizer import bill, energy diplomacy, and agricultural input costs simultaneously. The cost numbers are not yet proven at commercial scale, but the direction of travel matters — track this technology seriously over the next 24 months.
