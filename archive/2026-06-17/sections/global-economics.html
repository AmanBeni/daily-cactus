ECB's chief economist warns: the inflation from the Iran conflict is still loading — the pipeline isn't empty

ECB Chief Economist Philip Lane said policymakers must prepare for inflation that was unleashed by the Middle East conflict but hasn't fully fed through into prices yet. His colleague José Luis Escriva added that energy supply disruptions are likely to persist despite the ceasefire deal. Together, their comments suggest the ECB is more worried about second-wave inflation than a clean Hormuz-reopening reset would imply.

Why it matters: India watches ECB policy closely: a more hawkish ECB keeps European and global rates elevated, which compresses the RBI's room to cut even as India faces its own inflation-growth tension. The Iran ceasefire is not a macro clean slate — the inflationary transmission is still in motion.

China's economy is running two speeds at once — and the gap may be deliberate

A Bloomberg analysis examines China's unusual growth pattern: its export sector is booming while domestic consumer demand stalls. The piece asks whether this is a temporary imbalance that will self-correct or a deliberate geostrategic design — an economy optimised to accumulate industrial capacity and hard currency rather than to improve living standards.

Why it matters: If China's export boom is strategic rather than cyclical, Indian manufacturing competitiveness is the subject of a deliberate contest. The "China+1" supply chain shift benefits India, but only if Indian manufacturers can actually absorb demand — which requires policy continuity and infrastructure investment India's industry ministries are still fighting for.

Robinhood cuts 10% of staff; BlackRock trims near 1% — financial sector signals cost squeeze

Robinhood Markets said it is cutting approximately 300 positions — 10% of its workforce — as it restructures after a difficult post-2021 period. BlackRock is reportedly eliminating just under 1% of headcount. Both announcements arrived on the same day, spanning the fintech disruptor and the world's largest asset manager.

Why it matters: For Indian fintech founders benchmarking against global comps, this confirms growth-at-all-costs is definitively over. Unit economics will be the filter for the next funding cycle — and investors who backed Indian fintech on global comparisons will apply the same pressure domestically.

The economists most convinced AI will reshape the economy aren't in academia — they're at hedge funds and tech firms

The Economist profiles the cohort of economists who have gone furthest in arguing AI will produce transformative economic disruption: shorter work weeks, massive productivity gains, potential deflationary abundance. The piece finds them concentrated in industry and finance rather than university economics departments, which tend toward more cautious structural analysis.

Why it matters: The policy advice flowing to Indian government ministries about AI's economic impact will largely come from the "industry AI-pilled" cohort. Understanding the priors and incentives of those economists is necessary for reading their projections with appropriate calibration — or appropriate excitement.

Poland is aggressively courting Taiwanese tech investment to move up the value chain

Poland is actively recruiting Taiwanese semiconductor and electronics companies to invest in the country, aiming to move from a low-cost manufacturing base to a higher-tech economy. Poland sees Taiwan's supply chain diversification pressure as an opportunity to attract both capital and technology transfer.

Why it matters: India is playing the same game — the "China+1" and Taiwan diversification play was supposed to benefit India significantly. Poland's aggressive pursuit is a reminder that India faces competition for Taiwanese investment from well-governed, strategically located European alternatives. Industrial policy and ease of doing business remain decisive differentiators.
