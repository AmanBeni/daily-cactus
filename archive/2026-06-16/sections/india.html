India's May Exports Hit $45.2bn — Up 18% Year-on-Year, Even as Hormuz Was Closed

India's merchandise exports grew 18% year-on-year in May 2026 to $45.2 billion, driven by petroleum products (+54.89% on high prices), engineering goods (+24.48%), and electronics (+11.62%). The trade deficit widened to $28.21 billion as import costs stayed elevated. Combined April-May total exports (merchandise + services) reached $162.7 billion, up 14.66% — outpacing the same period last year by $20.8 billion.

Why it matters: Strong export growth while Hormuz was still closed signals genuine diversification and underlying global demand for Indian goods. With the strait now reopening, petroleum export earnings may normalize (high prices lifted that number artificially) — but engineering goods and electronics growth looks structurally sound, a much better signal for long-run competitiveness.

Hormuz Reopening Could Be India's Biggest Macro Tailwind of 2026

With the US-Iran interim peace deal signed June 14, India faces the direct inverse of the Hormuz crisis: fuel inflation that hit 30.33% in May could ease significantly by July CPI data, giving the RBI headroom to cut rates. India's FM had cited fuel-cost pressures as a key driver behind the widening trade deficit even as exports grew. The government had also exempted withholding tax on foreign investor G-sec interest to draw capital back. Earlier: RBI held rates at 5.25% and cut FY27 GDP forecast to 6.6%, citing stagflation risk from the energy shock.

Why it matters: India's stagflation risk was driven almost entirely by one external shock. If Hormuz stays open through the 60-day nuclear negotiation window, the macro environment for domestic startups, FMCG, logistics, and manufacturing all improve meaningfully. The June-July CPI prints become the key data to watch for an August RBI rate cut.

India's AI Mission: 6,500 Startups, AIKosh Platform, and ₹1,000 Cr for Chips — WEF Maps It

A World Economic Forum piece on India's AI-for-public-good approach documents the IndiaAI Mission (₹10,371 crore, 5 years), the AIKosh open platform (7,541 datasets and 273 AI models across 20 sectors available to developers), India's third-place global ranking in startup ecosystems, and Budget 2026-27's ₹1,000 crore allocation for India Semiconductor Mission 2.0 — targeting chip R&D and workforce development. India's AI governance sandbox allows startups to test AI solutions in healthcare, finance, and public services.

Why it matters: This is the clearest single-source map of India's public AI infrastructure for builders and founders. The AIKosh datasets and AI governance sandboxes are underused by private startups; the ISM 2.0 allocation signals sustained chip ambitions even as global semiconductor supply normalizes.
