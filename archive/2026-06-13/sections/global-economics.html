Strait of Hormuz Crisis Is a Full Commodity Shock — Oil at $97, Copper Breaking Records, Gas Up 25%

The Strait of Hormuz closure has cascaded into a broad commodity shock. WTI crude surged over 9% to above $94 per barrel; Brent passed $97. Copper broke above $6.40 per pound — the highest monthly close in its history — as supply chain disruption ripples into industrial metals. Natural gas jumped 25% last month. Analysts are now modelling $200 Brent scenarios if the closure spreads to pipeline routes.

Why it matters: When energy, metals, and gas move together, the shock is systemic — it doesn't stay contained in one sector. For India, the combined effect on manufacturing input costs, LPG import bills, and the current account balance compounds the stagflation risk the RBI flagged this week. This is the single biggest macro headwind of 2026 so far.

US Producer Prices Rising at Fastest Rate Since 2022 as Energy Costs Feed Through

The US Producer Price Index — tracking what businesses pay before costs reach consumers — rose at its fastest pace since 2022 in the latest reading, with elevated energy costs from the Hormuz crisis feeding through supply chains. Analysts are warning of second-round inflation effects: higher input costs becoming higher output prices, extending the inflationary period.

Why it matters: US business inflation eventually shows up in global goods prices, affecting India's import basket and trade balances. If PPI feeds into CPI, the Fed's rate path becomes more hawkish — strengthening the dollar and tightening the financial conditions for emerging market currencies, including the rupee. The RBI's neutral stance gets harder to hold.

IMF Cuts Global Growth Outlook to 2.5% for 2026 — Stagflation Scenarios Now Explicitly in View

The IMF's April 2026 World Economic Outlook projected global growth slowing to 2.5% in 2026 — a significant step down from earlier forecasts of 3.3%. Energy disruption, trade uncertainty, and policy volatility across major economies are cited as downside drivers. Stagflation scenarios are flagged as plausible for the first time in this economic cycle.

Why it matters: A 2.5% global growth rate is historically associated with near-recession conditions in parts of the developed world. For India's IT services, consulting, and global delivery workforce — the sector this reader emerged from — weak global GDP means slower client budget approvals, hiring freezes, and deferred projects. Context for FY27 career and business planning.

Emerging Market Bonds Outperforming Despite Global Turbulence — Investors Remain Constructive on EM

The macro backdrop for emerging markets remains "less volatile and more supportive" than the commodity shock might suggest, with investors constructive on EM local currency bonds, FX, and high-yield local debt. Strong US data — ISM Manufacturing and Services both beat expectations, employment held firm — reinforced USD strength without triggering an EM rout. The key risk remains escalating hostilities and further commodity disruption.

Why it matters: A constructive EM bond environment matters for India's external financing conditions — it means foreign investors are still willing to hold rupee-denominated assets despite global uncertainty. That provides a buffer against the currency pressure that would otherwise accompany India's rising import bills from the oil shock.
