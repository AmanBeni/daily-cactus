Bonn SB64 in Its Final Days: Negotiators Racing to Set the Table for COP31 in Antalya

The UNFCCC's 64th Subsidiary Body sessions (June 8–18, Bonn) are in their final stretch, covering mitigation, adaptation, climate finance, and technology transfer. A Climate and Trade Dialogue on June 13 brought UNCTAD and the International Trade Centre into the process for the first time, reflecting growing tension between CBAM-style border adjustments and developing-country trade concerns. The sessions are the formal halfway point between COP30 (Belém 2025) and COP31 (Antalya, Turkey, November 2026). Key unresolved issues: the Global Goal on Adaptation and the New Collective Quantified Goal on climate finance.

Why it matters: SB64 outcomes directly shape what gets tabled at COP31 — including the next round of nationally determined contributions India will file. The trade-climate linkage being raised here (CBAM, carbon border adjustments) will affect India's export competitiveness in steel, aluminium, and cement in ways that require corporate planning now, not after the policy is finalised. Worth tracking even when the headlines are procedural.

India Added 55.3 GW of Non-Fossil Power in FY26 — Now the World's Third-Largest Renewable Nation

India added 55.3 GW of non-fossil electricity capacity in FY 2025–26 — the country's largest annual renewable addition on record — bringing total non-fossil installed capacity to 283 GW. The breakdown: solar at 150 GW, wind at 56 GW, large hydro at 51 GW, plus nuclear and biopower. India crossed the 50% non-fossil share of total installed capacity in 2025 (ahead of its Paris Agreement NDC target) and is now the world's third-largest renewable nation by installed capacity. The 2030 target remains 500 GW.

Why it matters: The 55.3 GW addition in a single fiscal year is a structural story about India's energy economics, not a policy announcement. This much capacity is being deployed because renewable power is now commercially competitive without subsidies. The grid infrastructure gap — not generation capacity — is now India's binding constraint on the energy transition. For investors and strategy roles in energy, 283 GW is the baseline to plan from.
