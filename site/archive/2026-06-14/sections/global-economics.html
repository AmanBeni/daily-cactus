Hormuz Reopening Could End the Oil Shock — But the UAE Warns Full Flow Won't Resume Until 2027

A US-Iran peace deal mediated by Pakistan is expected to be signed imminently, with the Strait of Hormuz reopening as its centrepiece. Brent crude has been above $97/barrel since the strait's effective closure in February, following US-Israeli strikes on Iran. Insurance is currently unavailable for Hormuz transit, making the closure functionally total — 95% reduction in crude tanker movements, 99% in LNG. Pakistan PM Shehbaz Sharif called a deal "closer than ever." However, UAE state oil company estimates that even if a deal is signed immediately, full commercial shipping flows through the strait may not normalise until 2027 due to the insurance and logistics backlog. Earlier: RBI cited this energy shock as the primary external risk to India's FY27 inflation projection of 5.1%; Brent at $97 has compounded India's current account pressures.

Why it matters: A deal, even an imperfect one, would immediately ease the biggest macro headache facing India and most of Asia: the energy import cost feeding into CPI and holding the RBI in an uncomfortable wait-and-watch posture. The "2027 for full normalisation" caveat is important — the oil price relief will be partial and gradual. India-specific: cheaper LPG and crude restores fiscal and monetary headroom that currently doesn't exist, potentially opening space for RBI rate easing later in FY27.
