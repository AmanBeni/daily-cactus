Autism Isn't One Condition: Brain Scans Identify Two Biologically Distinct Subtypes

An international team analysing fMRI data from ~2,000 individuals — including 940 autistic people from the ABIDE dataset — found that autism clusters into two biologically distinct connectivity patterns: hypoconnectivity (reduced communication between brain regions, linked to synaptic dysfunction) and hyperconnectivity (elevated connectivity, linked to immune and transcriptional alterations). The finding was validated across 20 genetic mouse models, including teams from the Child Mind Institute and the Istituto Italiano di Tecnologia. Published in Nature Neuroscience.

Why it matters: One-size-fits-all autism interventions — whether behavioural, pharmacological, or educational — may be actively working against one of the two biological groups. The finding opens the door to biomarker-guided diagnosis and targeted therapy. This is also how the precision medicine revolution that transformed oncology begins in neurodevelopmental conditions: first, you sub-classify. Then, you target.

The Salk Institute Declares 2026 the Year of Brain Health — Here's What That Means in Practice

The Salk Institute for Biological Studies has declared 2026 its Year of Brain Health, consolidating research programmes across Alzheimer's disease, anxiety, neurodevelopmental disorders, sensory biology, and healthy ageing under a coordinated initiative. Active findings include: a newly identified Alzheimer's mechanism (mutations causing overly inflammatory brain immune cells, linking blood cancers to neurodegeneration); a small population of amygdala neurons whose restoration fully reverses anxiety and social deficits in mice. The Institute is also developing advanced brain organoid models for faster screening of neurological drug candidates.

Why it matters: Salk's Year of Brain Health is a useful annual reading list — they are among the world's best neuroscience institutes and their public communication is unusually clear. The Alzheimer's-blood-cancer immune link is a genuine surprise, suggesting current drug development may be missing a class of targets involving microglial dysregulation. Worth bookmarking if you follow neuroscience.
