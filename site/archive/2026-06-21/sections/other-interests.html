Faecal Transplant Makes the Brains of Old Mice Act Young Again

Older mice that received a faecal microbiome transplant from younger animals showed improved brain plasticity — their brains overcame a neurological stiffening that typically accompanies ageing. The transplant appears to restore gut microbiome diversity that correlates with neural flexibility, opening a new lens on the gut-brain axis in ageing.

Key takeaway: The gut microbiome may be modifiable to extend cognitive plasticity in old age — a striking proof of concept that the gut-brain axis is bidirectional and manipulable.

Why it matters: If the findings translate to humans, faecal microbiome transplants could eventually become a cognitive ageing intervention alongside drugs and lifestyle. It also raises questions about what our modern microbiome-depleting diets are costing us in brain health across the lifespan.

We've Found a Mysterious Substance on Titan and Pluto

Scientists have detected an unidentified substance that is absorbing light on the surfaces of both Saturn's moon Titan and Pluto. The same unknown material appearing on two very different worlds is puzzling — figuring out what it is could be key to understanding Titan's complex organic chemistry and potential habitability.

Key takeaway: The same unknown absorber on two very different worlds suggests it may form through a common chemical pathway — potentially a widespread organic process in the outer solar system.

Why it matters: Titan is one of the most Earth-like objects in the solar system, with lakes, rivers, and a thick nitrogen atmosphere — just with liquid methane instead of water. Understanding its surface chemistry could inform the search for prebiotic conditions beyond Earth. A genuinely mysterious discovery.

Dancing on Moonbeams: Artist Luke Fowler on Electronic Music Pioneer Janet Beat

The Quietus profiles Janet Beat, a largely unrecognised Scottish pioneer of electronic music who worked at the BBC Radiophonic Workshop from the 1970s and developed radical tape manipulation and synthesis techniques decades before they became mainstream. Artist Luke Fowler has created a new work celebrating her legacy.

Key takeaway: Janet Beat's story is a reminder that electronic music's lineage is fuller and stranger than the canonical histories — and that BBC institutional support enabled experiments no commercial label would have funded.

Why it matters: For anyone making or thinking about music production: the techniques Beat pioneered — tape splicing, frequency modulation, spectral layering — are the direct ancestors of DAW workflows. Knowing the history of where these tools came from changes how you hear them, and sometimes how you use them.
