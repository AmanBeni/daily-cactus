Warsh's Tenure at the Fed Begins — Rate Hikes Signal Growing Support

Kevin Warsh presided over his first Federal Reserve policy meeting as chair, vowing to restore price stability. Officials signalled growing support for interest-rate increases as the Fed's favourite inflation gauge is expected to show faster price growth. The consensus around hikes represents a significant hawkish shift under the new leadership.

Key takeaway: Warsh is opening his tenure hawkishly — the Fed under his leadership will tighten, not pause, as long as core inflation stays elevated.

Why it matters: A hiking Fed strengthens the dollar, tightens global financial conditions, and raises the cost of India's dollar-denominated external borrowing. It also attracts capital from emerging markets. The RBI will face pressure to either match (risking growth) or diverge (risking rupee depreciation and imported inflation). The Hormuz oil shock now makes the RBI's situation even more complex.

Fed's Favourite Gauge Seen Showing Faster Inflation

The latest PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures) inflation reading is expected to show acceleration — challenging any remaining "wait and see" arguments at the Fed. The data would reinforce the hawkish consensus building under Warsh and make near-term rate hikes highly probable.

Key takeaway: PCE trending upward plus a hawkish new Fed chair equals rate hikes coming — likely at the July or September meeting.

Why it matters: Every Fed hike is a headwind for emerging market equities and currencies. India's Sensex and the rupee have historically been sensitive to Fed tightening cycles. The compounding risk is that Hormuz-driven oil price pressure could simultaneously push India's own inflation higher, removing RBI's ability to cushion the impact with domestic rate cuts.

Trump's Fear of Economic Collapse Undercuts US Leverage in Iran Talks

Trump admitted that fear of global economic collapse from a prolonged Hormuz closure was a key reason he agreed to an interim deal with Iran. Bloomberg's analysis shows this admission exposes the core US weakness: America needs a deal more urgently than it has publicly acknowledged, which reduces negotiating leverage in Switzerland.

Key takeaway: When a president publicly admits economic fear drove a deal, the counterpart knows exactly what leverage they have in the next round.

Why it matters: If Iran believes the US cannot sustain economic pressure, the interim deal framework weakens. Any breakdown in Switzerland re-opens the Hormuz question — and India, with 40% of its crude imports transiting the Strait, would be among the first economies to feel the impact in oil prices and insurance costs.

Bolivia Declares State of Emergency After 50 Days of Unrest

Bolivian President Rodrigo Paz declared a state of emergency as security forces began clearing road blockades after 50 days of anti-government protests that severely disrupted the economy. The unrest has caused shortages of basic goods, fuel, and medicine in major cities.

Key takeaway: Bolivia's political crisis reflects a broader pattern of Latin American commodity-rich nations where resource revenues fail to reach the majority, generating persistent instability.

Why it matters: Bolivia holds the world's largest lithium reserves — critical for EV batteries. Political instability here directly affects lithium supply timelines and global EV economics, which India's Ola Electric, Ather, and battery manufacturing ambitions depend on.

How a Deal with the US Could Reconnect Iran to the Global Economy

If the US-Iran talks in Switzerland succeed, Iran's reintegration into global trade could reshape oil markets, regional supply chains, and sanctions architecture globally. Iran holds the world's fourth-largest oil reserves and second-largest gas reserves — reconnecting them would structurally ease global energy prices.

Key takeaway: A successful Iran deal would be deflationary for global oil prices — good for oil importers like India, challenging for OPEC+ producers.

Why it matters: India imports approximately 3-4% of its crude from Iran via informal channels despite sanctions. Full reintegration would open direct, cheaper supply lines and reduce the shipping cost premium India pays to route oil through intermediaries. Watch for early signals from the Switzerland talks.
