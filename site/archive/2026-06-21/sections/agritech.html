India Makes Historic Fieldays Debut, Signals New Chapter in India-NZ Ties Post-FTA

India participated for the first time in New Zealand's Fieldays — the Southern Hemisphere's largest agricultural event — showcasing farm technology, agritech innovations, and rural supply chain solutions. The participation follows the India-New Zealand Free Trade Agreement commitment and signals deeper bilateral engagement in agricultural trade and technology.

Key takeaway: India is actively marketing its agritech capabilities internationally — from precision irrigation to cold chain — building the export narrative alongside domestic deployment.

Why it matters: India's agritech sector serves 600 million farmers but exports almost nothing. Events like Fieldays open the door to export-oriented models, licensing partnerships, and foreign investment in Indian farm-tech — especially relevant for Rajasthan's water-stressed precision agriculture startups.

Landseed's Goal: Create a Financial Market for Conservation Projects

New company Landseed, led by Holdfast Collective's Greg Curtis, aims to make the full value of nature "visible, verifiable, and financially useful" — creating tradeable instruments for biodiversity credits, ecosystem services, and carbon from conservation projects. The model blends voluntary carbon markets with biodiversity finance.

Key takeaway: Conservation finance is evolving beyond carbon offsets into multi-dimensional nature credit markets — Landseed is attempting to build the infrastructure layer.

Why it matters: India has massive natural capital — forests, wetlands, grasslands — that is economically invisible under current accounting. If credible nature credit markets develop, India's forest-dependent communities and conservation agencies could monetise ecosystem stewardship rather than convert land. SEBI's ESG framework will eventually need to engage this space.
