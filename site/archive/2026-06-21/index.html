Iran Declares Hormuz Closed — and Heads to Switzerland to Talk

Iran announced it has closed the Strait of Hormuz, citing ongoing Israeli strikes in Lebanon as a ceasefire violation — even as negotiators flew to Switzerland for the next round of US-Iran talks. The US military disputed the closure claim. Trump threatened to impose tolls on Hormuz transit, adding a new lever to an already fragile standoff.

Key takeaway: Iran is using Hormuz as a pressure lever simultaneously with negotiation — pressure-while-talking is a classic Iranian posture, and the interim deal has clearly not resolved the underlying tensions.

Why it matters: India imports roughly 85% of its crude oil; around 70% transits Hormuz. Even a credible threat — not an actual closure — drives up war-risk insurance, delays tankers, and pushes Brent higher. That feeds directly into India’s inflation and current account at exactly the moment the RBI has already cut FY27 GDP to 6.6% and the Warsh Fed is threatening rate hikes. The two macro risks are converging. Earlier: US-Iran deal text finalized June 17; Hormuz was technically open but shippers were cautious, war-risk premiums elevated.

Nobel Laureate John Jumper Leaves DeepMind for Anthropic

John Jumper, who shared the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for AlphaFold’s protein-structure breakthroughs at Google DeepMind, is departing for Anthropic. He joins a string of senior DeepMind exits. TechCrunch notes he is not the only big name recently leaving Google’s AI lab.

Key takeaway: A sitting Nobel laureate chose Anthropic over every other lab — which is a stronger signal about research environment than any hiring announcement.

Why it matters: The AI talent war has moved beyond salary to research autonomy and mission. For tracking where the field’s next capability jumps will land, follow the top researchers — and right now they are moving toward Anthropic.

India’s Informal Workers Face the Heat — With Nowhere to Hide

India is piloting “parametric” heat insurance that pays out automatically when temperatures cross a threshold — no claim needed. But field evidence shows workers take the payout without actually resting, because missing income is impossible. The money transfers; the protection doesn’t.

Key takeaway: Parametric heat insurance is live in India but the product design fails at the human level: workers who cannot afford to stop working will not, even when paid to.

Why it matters: With 95 of the world’s hottest cities currently in India and hundreds of millions of outdoor workers, this is a wicked problem that markets alone won’t solve. Serious climate-tech and social insurance innovation is increasingly focused here.
