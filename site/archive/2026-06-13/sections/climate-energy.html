Every Major Energy Model Agrees: The 1.5°C Climate Target Is Gone

Resources for the Future's 2026 Global Energy Outlook harmonizes 15 scenarios across eight major organizations — IEA, BP, Shell, ExxonMobil, IRENA, OPEC, TotalEnergies, and the US EIA — and concludes that limiting warming to 1.5°C is "no longer plausible." Wind and solar more than double across all scenarios, but global emissions are only forecast to peak by 2030–35, leaving even a 2°C ceiling "extremely challenging."

Why it matters: This is the scientific and energy establishment's equivalent of a unanimous jury verdict. For India, it means every long-term infrastructure, water security, and city-planning decision now needs adaptation baked in as a certainty, not a contingency. The game is simultaneously decarbonizing and building resilience against a warmer world — both at once.

Hormuz Blockade Pushes Oil Past $97 and Natural Gas Up 25% — Worst Energy Shock Since 2022

With the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed since February 28, WTI crude has surged past $94 per barrel and Brent above $97. Natural gas jumped more than 15% last week and ended the month roughly 25% higher overall. Analysts are now modelling scenarios in which Brent could reach $200 if the Iran conflict expands to pipeline routes and the closure becomes permanent.

India angle: Around 84% of the crude oil and 83% of the LNG transiting Hormuz goes to Asian nations — India among the largest recipients. India's LPG imports (60% of demand through this route) are already showing supply delays and price pressure. This is not a geopolitical abstraction for India; it is a kitchen-table energy security crisis.

UNEP Says Clean Technologies Have Crossed the Competitiveness Line — They Are Now the Cheap Option

For World Environment Day (June 5), the UN Environment Programme released a policy brief titled "Cheaper. Cleaner. Unstoppable." arguing that solar power, electric mobility, and sustainable cooling have crossed from niche alternatives to economically competitive mainstream options — often now the cheapest choice available. The brief identifies grids and EV charging infrastructure as the largest remaining deployment gap, not power generation itself.

Why it matters: The shift from "green premium" to "clean is cheapest" changes the economics of every energy decision in India. The grid bottleneck finding is particularly relevant: India is adding renewable generation at record pace but grid infrastructure investment is lagging, creating curtailment and reliability problems that are slowing the transition's full benefits.

The Energy Transition's Missing Investment Is in Grids, Not Power Plants

Wood Mackenzie's 2026 energy transition investment analysis finds that the largest spending gap has shifted: it is no longer in power generation — renewables are being deployed at record pace — but in enabling infrastructure: electricity grids, grid-scale storage, and EV charging networks. Without these, additional renewable capacity sits stranded or curtailed, unable to reach end users.

India angle: India is adding solar and wind capacity aggressively, but grid infrastructure investment is visibly lagging — transmission bottlenecks and curtailment are already reported in high-renewable states. For infrastructure investors and policymakers, this is where the next decade's capital allocation challenge lies.

EU Approves €23 Billion Italian Renewable Support Scheme — 37 GW of New Clean Capacity

The European Commission cleared a €23 billion Italian state aid scheme on June 9 to support new onshore wind, solar, hydro, and sewage-gas installations, adding 37.15 gigawatts to Italy's renewable portfolio. The announcement came during the European Sustainable Energy Week in Brussels, which ran under the theme "A Clean, Secure and Competitive Energy Union."

Why it matters: Context for the world you operate in: Europe is putting serious fiscal firepower behind its energy transition — the scale of public subsidy being deployed internationally sets a benchmark for what India may need to mobilize through both public and blended finance to meet its own 500 GW renewable target by 2030.
