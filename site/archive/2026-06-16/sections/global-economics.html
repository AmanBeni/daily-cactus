Hormuz Reopening: What the US-Iran Deal Means for Oil Markets and Shipping Costs

Bloomberg's June 15 analysis finds the Hormuz reopening will gradually reverse the energy price spike — but the pace depends on how quickly tanker rerouting unwinds, OPEC+ production responses, and whether Iran's 60-day nuclear talks succeed. Before the February blockade, 25% of global seaborne oil and 20% of LNG moved through the strait. Brent was near $97 at peak; analysts expect a correction toward the low-$80s if the deal holds through August. Earlier: US and Israel launched strikes on Iran February 28; Hormuz effectively closed for 108 days.

Why it matters: India's import bill, rupee trajectory, and RBI's rate-cut window all hinge on where crude settles. A Brent correction toward $80 gives the RBI room to cut in August 2026, which affects startup funding conditions, FMCG margins, and logistics economics across India. The macro case for India just improved materially.

ECB June Report: CIPS Surged 33% After Iran War Outbreak, Stablecoins Hit $300B Market Cap

The ECB's June 2026 report on the international role of the euro documents two structural shifts: CIPS (China's cross-border payment system) settlement activity increased by more than one-third in the days following the Iran war outbreak, with analysts suggesting the conflict accelerated renminbi's role in energy markets. Separately, stablecoin market capitalization crossed $300 billion — 50% above 2024 — following the US Genius Act's framework for payment stablecoins.

Why it matters: Two geopolitical tailwinds for dollar alternatives arriving simultaneously: CIPS expansion (petrodollar diversification accelerated by war) and regulated stablecoins (Genius Act gives payment stables legal footing). Neither threatens dollar dominance near-term, but both are structural shifts in global payments that matter over a 5-10 year horizon for Indian fintech, trade finance, and cross-border payments.

KKR Mid-Year Macro: "The Divergence Conundrum" — No More Synchronized Easing

KKR's mid-year outlook argues the global central bank easing cycle has fragmented: advanced economies face slow growth while a handful of emerging markets with strong reform momentum are pulling ahead. Country risk spreads fell from 2,500 bps (late 2023) to ~600 bps (end-2025), with the Hormuz shock partially reversing gains. Argentina is cited as one emerging market likely to regain market access in 2026; KKR flags that domestic inflation and fiscal credibility — not Fed moves — now drive individual country rate paths.

Why it matters: If global easing is diverging rather than converging, the RBI has less cover to cut rates by citing Fed moves. India's August rate decision depends on domestic inflation data — and with Hormuz reopening, June-July CPI prints will be the key variable. If fuel inflation corrects sharply, the RBI may have room; if the nuclear talks fail and oil spikes again, it doesn't.
