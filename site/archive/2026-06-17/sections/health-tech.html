A startup is repurposing failed clinical trial data to build a smarter AI patient-matching model for neurology

Verge Labs has developed an AI model that uses data from past failed clinical trials to improve patient stratification for neurology studies — essentially identifying which patients are most likely to respond to a given treatment. The company pitches this as turning a "waste product" of the drug development industry into a precision enrollment tool.

Why it matters: Clinical trial failure is catastrophically expensive — the industry loses billions to studies that fail because the wrong patients were enrolled. If AI patient stratification delivers even modest improvements in trial success rates, it reshapes neurological drug development economics. India is a major site for global clinical trials; this technology has direct application here.

Medicare will cover weight-loss drugs starting next month under a "temporary" programme that may become permanent

Weight-loss drugs — GLP-1 agonists including Ozempic and Wegovy — will be available through Medicare from next month under a pilot designed to be temporary but widely expected to be very difficult to wind down once running. The programme covers tens of millions of eligible seniors and represents the US health system's most significant endorsement of these drugs yet.

Why it matters: GLP-1 drugs are reshaping how health systems worldwide think about obesity, diabetes, and metabolic disease. When Medicare covers them, it signals that the US has effectively decided these drugs are standard of care. Indian health insurers and CGHS are watching; the pricing and reimbursement debate that follows in India will be significant for both pharma and insurance sectors.

Merck commits $510 million to AI drug design startup Protillion

Merck & Co. has signed a $510 million biobucks collaboration with Protillion, an AI-driven drug design specialist. Under the deal, Protillion's platform will be paired with Merck's drug discovery expertise to generate new molecular candidates. The "biobucks" structure means most of the value is milestone-contingent — Merck pays only if Protillion's candidates succeed in trials.

Why it matters: Big pharma is now committing hundreds of millions to AI-driven drug discovery — well beyond pilot partnerships. For India's biotech ecosystem and anyone tracking the convergence of AI and life sciences, this scale of commitment signals that AI drug discovery is no longer experimental infrastructure but a core R&D channel.

FDA approves Colorado's plan to import cheaper prescription drugs from Canada

The FDA has approved Colorado's plan to import certain prescription drugs from Canada, making it the first US state to receive federal authorisation for such a programme. The initiative gives Colorado residents access to drugs at Canadian regulated prices, which are substantially lower than US market rates for the same molecules.

Why it matters: Drug price arbitrage between countries is politically charged but economically rational. India lives this reality on the export side — Indian generic manufacturers supply much of the world below branded equivalents' prices. The US importing from Canada is that logic in reverse, arriving in the world's largest drug market, with implications for global pharma pricing architecture and India's export strategy.
