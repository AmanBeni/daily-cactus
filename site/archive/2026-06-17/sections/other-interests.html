The Secrets to Keeping Your Brain Sharp Into Old Age

Neurologist Emily Rogalski has spent a decade studying "superagers" — people in their 80s and 90s with memories as sharp as people thirty years younger. Her research finds that the common factors are not puzzles or brain games but rather intense social engagement, physical activity with a cardiovascular component, and crucially, a high tolerance for discomfort and challenge rather than cognitive ease.

Key takeaway: The brain stays sharp through challenge and connection, not comfort — passive mental activity like TV doesn't count; effortful engagement does.

Why it matters: India's rapidly ageing population (projected 320 million over 60 by 2050) makes healthy cognitive ageing a major public health priority. This research suggests the answer is social and physical, not pharmaceutical — a finding with implications for elder care policy and design of senior living environments.

Autism May Have Two Distinct Subtypes That Differ in Brain Activity Patterns

New research provides some of the strongest evidence yet that autism is not a single condition but at least two distinct subtypes with different neural signatures — one characterised by heightened brain connectivity and one by reduced connectivity. The subtypes respond differently to interventions, which could explain why some autism therapies work well for some people and poorly for others.

Key takeaway: Autism treatments have been one-size-fits-all because we assumed it was one condition — it isn't, and subtype-specific treatment is the next frontier.

Why it matters: India has an estimated 18 million people on the autism spectrum, with significant underdiagnosis especially in rural areas. Subtype-specific diagnosis and intervention could dramatically improve outcomes — but requires diagnostic infrastructure that most Indian states don't yet have.

A New Species of Walking Shark Has Been Found in Papua New Guinea

Scientists have identified Hemiscyllium dudgeonae — the tenth recorded species of walking shark — from Papua New Guinea's coral reefs. Walking sharks use their pectoral fins to "walk" across reef flats during low tide, and are found only in a small area of the Indo-Pacific. The species has a limited range, making it immediately vulnerable to habitat destruction and climate-driven reef bleaching.

Key takeaway: A walking shark discovered in 2026 is a reminder that ocean biodiversity surveys are still finding entirely new vertebrate species — we know less about our oceans than we think.

Why it matters: India's Lakshadweep and Andaman coral ecosystems are home to similarly undocumented species. As climate bleaching accelerates, India risks losing reef biodiversity before it's even catalogued — an irreversible scientific and ecological loss.

Arctic Ocean Has Reached a Tipping Point That Threatens Marine Life

Disappearing sea ice is letting far more sunlight into the Arctic Ocean than at any point in recorded history, triggering a phytoplankton bloom that has depleted a crucial nutrient — silica — from the upper ocean. Without silica, diatoms that form the base of the Arctic food chain cannot grow, threatening a cascade of population collapses from krill to whales.

Key takeaway: The Arctic has crossed a tipping point — not just losing ice, but losing the nutrient chemistry that sustains its food web.

Why it matters: Arctic marine collapse affects global fish stock distributions, including fish that migrate through Indian Ocean waters. It also signals that we are further into irreversible climate tipping points than most models assumed — a sobering update to the baseline for Indian climate adaptation planning.

55% of Travellers Would Visit a Place Because of a Book — Literary Tourism Is Booming

A new survey by Book Riot finds that 55% of travellers have considered visiting a location because they read about it in a book — and the trend of "literary tourism" has grown significantly post-pandemic, with dedicated literary trails, author house museums, and fiction-inspired itineraries becoming a meaningful segment of the travel market.

Key takeaway: Books are becoming travel infrastructure — places with strong literary associations are seeing measurable tourism uplift from readers who want to walk in fictional footsteps.

Why it matters: India has extraordinary literary geography — from Ruskin Bond's Dehradun to R.K. Narayan's Malgudi to Amitav Ghosh's Calcutta. A more intentional literary tourism strategy could drive high-value, culturally engaged visitors to destinations outside the Golden Triangle circuit.
