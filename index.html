Sarvam AI Becomes India's Newest Unicorn With $234 Million HCLTech-Led Round

Sarvam AI, the Bengaluru startup building India-specific large language models and speech technology, has raised $234 million in a round led by HCLTech — crossing the billion-dollar valuation mark. The funding backs plans to build advanced AI models, expand compute infrastructure, and strengthen what the company calls India's sovereign AI ecosystem. Razorpay, meanwhile, has confidentially filed its draft red herring prospectus, signalling the fintech is finally ready for a public markets debut.

Key takeaway: India now has a funded, home-grown AI unicorn with a clear sovereign-AI mandate — something no other developing economy has pulled off at this scale.

Why it matters: Sarvam's rise matters on two levels. First, it validates the bet that language models trained on Indian languages (Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, Kannada) and Indian contexts can outperform generic LLMs for India's 1.4 billion users. Second, HCLTech's lead cheque signals that large Indian IT firms — long seen as services businesses — are willing to own equity in the AI infrastructure layer. If Sarvam succeeds, it could become the spine of India Stack 2.0.

Jio Files IPO Papers Before Reliance AGM — Potentially India's Biggest-Ever Listing

Reliance Jio is expected to file its draft red herring prospectus with SEBI before Reliance Industries' AGM on Friday, formally launching what could be India's largest IPO in history. Jio's telecom business alone serves over 480 million subscribers, and analysts have valued the entity at upwards of $100 billion.

Key takeaway: India's retail investor base is about to be offered a piece of the country's dominant digital infrastructure play.

Why it matters: A Jio listing would dwarf every prior Indian IPO, potentially unlocking wealth for millions of retail investors while also testing whether domestic capital markets can absorb a company of this size without foreign anchor dependency.

Bank of Japan Raises Rates to 31-Year High of 1% as Iran War Inflation Bites

The Bank of Japan raised its benchmark rate to 1% — the highest since 1995 — citing persistent inflationary pressure worsened by the Iran-Hormuz energy shock. The US Federal Reserve and Bank of England held rates steady. BOJ action marks a decisive end to Japan's era of ultra-loose monetary policy.

Key takeaway: The last major holdout for negative-rate policy has now joined the global tightening cycle, triggered in part by Middle East energy disruption.

Why it matters: Higher Japanese rates unwind the yen carry trade — borrowed yen invested in higher-yielding emerging markets — potentially triggering capital outflows from markets including India. Every 25 bps BOJ move has historically caused volatility in EM currencies and equities.

For Traumatised Indian Sailors, Hormuz Reopening Brings Little Relief

Captain Raman Kapoor and hundreds of Indian merchant mariners describe PTSD-like symptoms after months operating through a war zone — convoy escorts, blackout nights, and missile alerts. Even as the US-Iran truce reopens the Strait of Hormuz, maritime insurers are still charging war-zone premiums and many crews are waiting for normalcy that hasn't arrived.

Key takeaway: Hormuz is technically open but trauma, elevated insurance costs, and slow repatriation mean the human crisis in Indian shipping is far from over.

Why it matters: India has the world's largest merchant seafarer workforce — over 200,000 active sailors. Their mental health and legal protections are now a live policy question touching the Ministry of Ports and the Directorate General of Shipping.

Energy-Efficient ACs Could Save Indian Households ₹69 Billion a Year

A CarbonBrief analysis finds that shifting India's air conditioning stock to 5-star BEE-rated units could save households ₹69 billion ($724 million) in electricity costs annually. With India's AC penetration expected to surge from 10% today toward 40% by 2040, the efficiency standards set now will determine decades of energy demand.

Key takeaway: Appliance standards, not carbon taxes, are the most tractable Indian climate lever — and the savings belong to consumers, not governments.

Why it matters: India is on course to see the largest AC adoption boom in human history. If it mirrors China's low-efficiency path, peak summer power demand will overwhelm grids. If it mandates 5-star, the savings rival a medium-sized renewable project — without subsidies.

PBOC Hints at Fed-Style Rate Framework, Focusing on Overnight Rate

China's central bank signaled a potential shift toward an overnight policy rate as its primary monetary policy tool — a move that would bring the PBOC in line with global peers including the US Federal Reserve and European Central Bank. The change would increase rate-setting transparency and could accelerate the yuan's bid for global reserve currency status.

Key takeaway: China is quietly building the monetary policy architecture needed for yuan internationalisation — borrowing the Fed's most credible tool.

Why it matters: A more predictable PBOC framework could reshape how Asian trade — including India-China — is invoiced and settled. It also reduces the surprise-intervention risk that has historically made yuan-denominated contracts unattractive for Indian exporters.
